# The Plumber Who Knew Too Much

There is a particular kind of confidence that comes from watching someone do a job.

You have seen enough YouTube videos. You have read the forums. You know, roughly, how pipes work — how water moves through a house, where pressure builds, why things leak. And so when the plumber arrives, you are ready. Not to stand back. To *help*.

"Try that pipe first," you tell him, pointing. He looks at it. He knows, in the way that a man with twenty years of burst mains and frozen joints and corroded elbows knows, that the pipe is fine. But you are insistent. So he tries the pipe.

"Now tighten that fitting on the left." He tightens it. "What about the pressure valve — shouldn't we check the pressure valve?" He checks the pressure valve.

An hour passes. The leak remains.

You pay the bill and tell your neighbor the plumber was no good.

---

Here is what actually happened in that bathroom.

The plumber walked in and noticed something you did not: a faint mineral stain on the wall, six inches above the floor, slightly behind the cabinet. In his experience — and he has seen this exact pattern a dozen times — that stain means a slow weep from a joint hidden inside the wall. Not dramatic. Not obvious. The kind of thing that takes months to show itself.

He never got to look at it. You sent him left, and he went left.

This is not a story about a bad plumber. It is a story about what happens when we confuse *access* to information with *expertise*. When we have learned just enough to feel confident directing, but not enough to know what we are missing.

The psychologists have a name for this. David Dunning and Justin Kruger documented it in 1999: the less we know about a domain, the less equipped we are to recognize the gaps in our own knowledge. Competence, it turns out, is partly the ability to see your own incompetence. Novices cannot do this. So they direct. They point at pipes.

---

Now consider what you do when you open a new AI tool.

You have probably done this. You type a careful, specific prompt. You tell it exactly what format you want. You specify the structure, the length, the approach. You are, in a very real sense, telling the plumber which pipe to check.

And the AI — trained on more text than any human could read in a thousand lifetimes, having seen the patterns underneath patterns underneath patterns — does exactly what you tell it. It goes left.

This is the thing most people do not understand about these systems. The risk is not that AI will do too much. The risk is that *you* will do too much. That your instructions, well-meaning and logical as they feel, will fence the model away from the solution it would have found on its own.

The best prompts are not the most detailed ones. They are the ones that describe the *outcome* — what a working system looks like — and then get out of the way.

Tell the plumber: *I need this house to stop leaking*. Not: *check the third fitting on the left*.

---

What the expert does, and what the novice does not, is verify rather than direct.

The plumber fixes the joint inside the wall. You turn on the tap. You watch. You check the floor, the cabinet, the pressure. If something is still wrong, you say: *still leaking under the sink*. You do not say: *tighten the bolt on the left side of the P-trap a quarter turn counterclockwise*.

This is the correct relationship with expertise. You own the outcome. They own the method. And when it works — when the system finally holds pressure and the floor stays dry — you can ask: *what did you find, and why did you fix it that way?* A good plumber will tell you. A good AI will too. In more detail than you probably expected.

The loop stays closed. You stay informed. You just stopped pretending to be something you are not.

---

There is a version of every tool that makes you better at using it, and a version that makes you worse.

With AI, the trap is seductive because partial knowledge feels like full knowledge. You have used it enough to get *decent* results by directing it closely. What you have never seen is what it would have built if you had simply told it the problem and stepped back — because you never tried.

The plumber knew where the leak was the moment he walked in.

You just never let him show you.
